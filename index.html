Why do anything

The cost of the status quo

  • A 21-day ransomware recovery could expose AMD to an estimated $1.3B loss; downtime alone risks ~$65M/day.
  • Legacy tooling is slow and blind — semiconductor recoveries have historically dragged on for weeks.
  • Fragmented recovery, threat-hunting and detection tools add time and complexity.
  • Infrastructure without MFA, immutability or network isolation invites breach, data loss and reinfection.
Why Rubrik

One platform, built zero-trust

  • Unified, self-service protection across VMs, databases, NAS, cloud and SaaS — one interface.
  • Rapid recovery: 1,000+ VMs in under 20 minutes, database Live Mounts in under 10.
  • Native immutability with mandatory MFA, encryption, retention lock and two-person approval.
  • Detect, isolate and quarantine threats, then restore a known-clean copy — weeks to under 48 hours.
  • Content-aware data reduction cuts storage 25–40% and retires redundant tools.

04 — DISCOVERY → DELIVERED

Sized for growth, tracking to plan.

The February 2025 purchase sized each cluster for the discovery scope plus 10% annual growth, at 2.5:1 data reduction for a one-full-plus-30-day incremental-forever schedule. Sixteen months on, that's ~14% of built-in growth — and measured by Used Size, the dedicated clusters are protecting ~1.1× their growth-adjusted plan, with capacity headroom still intact.

Atlanta is the headline. Scoped at 1,306 TB in Feb 2025 — ~1,488 TB once grown 10%/yr — it now protects 1,653 TB by Used Size, running ahead of plan, yet sits at just 68% capacity. Sized for growth, and absorbing it without a forced expansion.
Colorado is the North American DR target. Every NA site replicates to it, so it was sized for the regional aggregate (~2,292 TB). As a DR replication target its objects report physical storage but not Used Size, so its protected volume can't be measured in this column — an expected reporting exception, not a gap in protection.
By design, not everything got hardware. ~11 small satellite sites (≈84 TB) — Sunnyvale, Boston, Santa Clara, Orlando and others — were deemed too small for dedicated clusters and are protected over WAN. A further ~334 TB of Azure and GCP workloads are protected cloud-native, and the Dublin (Ireland) cluster (≈75 TB scoped) is the final deployment site, due Sep 2026.
China is a deliberate reporting exception. Suzhou, Shanghai and Beijing (≈77 TB scoped) run RSC-P — Rubrik Security Cloud Private — deployed on in-country VMs to satisfy Chinese data-sovereignty law, which prohibits SaaS-based management from outside China. Protected capacity there is not centrally reported, so it sits outside the figures above as an ongoing exception — meaning the true protected total is higher than the ~3.1 PB shown.
One to validate: San Jose currently reports below its discovery scope by Used Size (209 TB vs 422 TB). Worth confirming whether the original scope included planned growth, or whether workloads have since been consolidated or moved to the Colorado DR target.

Markham is the clearest cleanup win. At 84% it's the only cluster flagged for expansion — but ~39 TB of its footprint is unprotected and Do Not Protect residual. Reclaiming it drops utilization to ~73%, deferring a hardware expansion the customer doesn't yet need. The weekly notes already recommend exactly this cleanup. A note on the capacity math. Protected footprint here uses each object's Local Storage — cross-checked against the cluster's reported utilization in the CRM report, it reconciles within ~2% across all 14 clusters, whereas the Used Size column does not (it is unpopulated for Colorado and over- or under-states elsewhere).
